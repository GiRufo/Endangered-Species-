The Maned Wolf -- Chrysocyon Brachyurus



Relatives?

The maned wolf, an animal closely resembling a fox, is neither a fox nor a wolf, despite what the name implies. In fact, the maned wolf in not related to any other canids, the biological family of dogs. Because it has no close, definite relations, it is the only species in genus Chrysocyon. The main thing that sets the Maned Wolf apart from a fox is its lack of elliptical pupils.



Where is the Maned Wolf?

The manned wolf can typically be found in areas with a plethora of vegetation, typically in the grasslands of southeastern Brazil, Paraguay, northern Argentina, Bolivia, and far southeastern Peru, surrounded by bushes and trees. It is considered a " vulnerable species " due to loss of habitat in Uruguay, resulting in the displacement of this now rare mammal, though still considered " lower risk " by the IUCN, the International Union for the Conservation of Nature



What does it look like?

The Maned Wolf is the tallest of wild canids, with long legs thought to be inherited from the tall grasslands of their habitat, and allowing them to stand, on average, at 35 inches. The average adult weighs 51 pounds, has an 18 inch long tail, and ears that are tall and long, typically 7 inches. Their fur can be many different hues from reddish brown to a golden orange. They have long black legs and a distinct black mane. They are known for having a white tuft of hair at the tip of their tail as well as a white patch, or " bib " underneath their throat. Maned Wolf litters have from two to six black-furred pups that are fully grown by ten months and can take care of themselves by the end of their first year.





Habits and Notable Personalities

Unlike other canids, the maned wolf is known for not forming packs and feasting on small animals. These large predators hunt alone between sundown and midnight for prey such as rodents, hares, and birds. To locate their prey in the grass the Maned Wolf rotates its large ears to listen carefully for any rustling. Once they hear their meal they tap the ground with their foot, making the other animal jump from its hiding spot, and thus allowing for the Maned Wolf to grab it in its mouth. While this way of getting food is violent and bloody, the Maned Wolf is known for surprising researchers with the discovery that over 50% of the Maned Wolf's diet is made of fruits like the Wolf Apple, a tomato-like food, as well as vegetation, making the Maned Wolf an omnivorous creature.



The Maned Wolf's Relationships with Humans

The Maned Wolf, though described in this page as being a hunter that attacks small animals, poses virtually no threat to humans. Though they have been know as " chicken thieves " for stealing chickens, cattle, and sheep from farms in this past, this has been proven false and we now know that when met by humans this shy animal will typically flee upon being alarmed.



Is the Maned Wolf in Danger?

Historically, many Brazilian hunters tracked down the Maned Wolf for its eyes, thought to be good luck charms. Since its classification as a vulnerable species though, they are now protected by the Brazilian government. Habitat loss is still a threat, as well as the danger of the wolves being run over by cars. Illness from domestic dogs, most notably infections by the giant kidney worm, are fatal, and besides the spreading of this disease Maned Wolves have been known to be attacked by both domestic and feral dogs. The Smithsonian National Zoo Park has been working to protect the wolves for nearly 3 decades by studying them in the wild, protecting their habitat, and educating people about them. FUN FACTS

  • Scientific Name: Chrysocyon brachyurus
  • Kingdom: Animalia
  • Phylum: Chordata
  • Subphylum: Vertebrata
  • Class: Mammalia
  • Order: Carnivora
  • Life Span: 12 to 15 years
  • Natural Habitat: Dry, shrubby forests and grasslands
  • Call: Roar-bark, growls and high-pitched wine
  • Maned wolves were the only canids species that survived the late Pleistocene extinction of South American canids.
  • Study of its DNA published in 2009, revealed that maned wolves are related to the extinctFalkland Islandswolves because both of them share one common ancestor that existed about 6 million years ago.
  • The Bush dog is one of the living canids species that resembles the maned wolf.
